\subsection*{S1\quad Prompt Used for Single-Agent RoB Assessment}
\label{sec:si-prompt}

The following prompt was used without modification across all eight models and all
227~studies.
Each study was assessed in seven sequential inference calls---one per RoB domain---with
the full Markdown text of the study report included in every call as the user message,
followed by the domain identifier (e.g.\ \texttt{Domain: D1}).
The prompt text below was supplied as the system message.

\small
\begin{verbatim}
Goal: Systematically assess the risk of bias in a research article using
Cochrane RoB 1.0. Evaluate bias domain, assign a judgment (Low, High,
or Unclear Risk), and justify strictly from the article evidence.

---

Preliminary Considerations

Before assessing, identify:
1. Intervention Type: Pharmacological | Surgical/Procedural |
   Non-pharmacological | Device
2. Primary Outcome Type: Objective (mortality, lab values) |
   Subjective (pain, QoL)
3. Comparator: Placebo | Active comparator | Usual care | No intervention

Critical Context: Blinding expectations vary by intervention type.
For pharmacological interventions, lack of placebo is a serious flaw.
For surgical/non-pharmacological interventions where blinding is impossible,
focus on compensatory measures: blinding of outcome assessors and objective
endpoints.

---

Bias Domains

1. Random Sequence Generation (Selection Bias)
   Signalling Question: Was a truly random method used?
   Low Risk:    Random method adequately described (computer-generated,
                random tables, coin toss, dice, shuffled envelopes,
                minimization with random element)
   High Risk:   Non-random method (alternation, date of birth, hospital
                record number, clinician judgment)
   Unclear Risk: States "randomized" but no method details provided

2. Allocation Concealment (Selection Bias)
   Signalling Question: Was the allocation sequence concealed from those
   enrolling participants until assignment?
   Low Risk:    Adequate concealment (central/telephone randomization,
                sequentially numbered opaque sealed envelopes, numbered
                drug containers, independent pharmacy)
   High Risk:   Allocation not concealed or predictable (open list,
                non-opaque envelopes, alternation)
   Unclear Risk: Insufficient information to judge
   Note: Allocation concealment (before randomization) is different from
   blinding (after randomization).

3. Blinding of Participants and Personnel (Performance Bias)
   Signalling Questions: Were participants/personnel aware of assignments?
   If yes, could this affect outcomes?
   Pharmacological: Double-blinding expected; no placebo implies higher
   risk for subjective outcomes.
   Surgical/Non-pharmacological: Blinding may be impossible and is not
   automatically High Risk; check compensatory safeguards.
   Objective outcomes: Less susceptible to performance bias.
   Low Risk:    Adequate blinding, OR blinding impossible with compensatory
                measures, OR outcome is objective/hard endpoint
   High Risk:   No blinding + subjective outcome + no compensatory measures
   Unclear Risk: Insufficient blinding information

4. Blinding of Outcome Assessors (Detection Bias)
   Signalling Questions: Were assessors blinded? Is outcome objective or
   subjective?
   Low Risk:    Assessors blinded, OR objective outcome unlikely to be
                influenced by assignment knowledge
   High Risk:   Assessors not blinded + subjective outcome requiring
                interpretation
   Unclear Risk: Insufficient information
   Note: For patient-reported outcomes, participant acts as assessor.

5. Incomplete Outcome Data (Attrition Bias)
   Signalling Questions: Are data available for most participants? Are
   missing-data reasons balanced/unrelated to outcome?
   Low Risk:    Minimal missingness (<5%), OR balanced missingness with
                unrelated reasons, OR valid ITT/imputation
   High Risk:   High missingness (>10-20%), OR imbalance between groups,
                OR outcome-related missingness, OR per-protocol exclusions
   Unclear Risk: Missingness not adequately reported

6. Selective Reporting (Reporting Bias)
   Signalling Questions: Is protocol/registry available? Were pre-specified
   outcomes reported?
   Low Risk:    All pre-specified outcomes reported, OR protocol/registry
                aligns with publication
   High Risk:   Selective non-reporting (methods outcomes missing in
                results, changed primary outcome, significance-driven
                reporting)
   Unclear Risk: No protocol/registry and insufficient information

7. Other Bias
   Signalling Questions: Are there other bias sources (early stopping,
   baseline imbalance, conflicts, contamination)?
   Low Risk:    No relevant additional bias detected
   High Risk:   Important additional bias present
   Unclear Risk: Insufficient information

---

Key Principles
1. Evidence-based only: Base judgments on explicit information. Do not
   assume. If evidence is missing, choose "Unclear Risk".
2. Cite evidence: Reference specific text or note absence of information.
3. Context matters: Apply blinding expectations appropriate to intervention
   type.
4. Impossibility vs Negligence: Not blinding when impossible differs from
   not blinding when feasible.
5. Outcome sensitivity: Subjective outcomes require stricter blinding than
   objective ones.

---

Return ONLY valid JSON with:
{
  "judgement": "Low Risk" | "High Risk" | "Unclear Risk",
  "justification": "short justification"
}
\end{verbatim}
\normalsize

\subsection*{S7\quad Confidence Interval Method Comparison}
\label{sec:si-ci}

All confidence intervals reported in the main text were computed using
study-clustered bootstrap (5{,}000 iterations, resampling the 227 studies
with replacement).
This section documents the comparison between bootstrap and two Wilson score
interval variants for accuracy, and explains why the bootstrap approach was
adopted for all metrics.

\subsubsection*{Wilson score interval}

For a proportion $p$ observed over $n$ independent Bernoulli trials,
the Wilson 95\% CI is
\begin{equation}
  \left(
    \frac{n p + \tfrac{z^2}{2}}{n + z^2}
    \;\pm\;
    \frac{z\,\sqrt{n p(1-p) + \tfrac{z^2}{4}}}{n + z^2}
  \right),
  \label{eq:wilson}
\end{equation}
with $z = 1.96$.
Applied to accuracy ($p \approx 0.60$), two natural choices for $n$ exist:
$n = 1{,}589$ (all annotated cells, assuming independence) or
$n = 227$ (studies as the primary sampling unit).

\subsubsection*{Intraclass correlation and effective sample size}

The 1{,}589 cells are not independent: seven domain judgements are drawn from
the same study.
Pearson's one-way intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) for the
correct/wrong binary indicator across the seven domains within each study
was estimated at $\hat\rho = 0.044$ (computed for Claude~Haiku~4.5 as a
representative model).
The low ICC indicates that whether a model correctly judges one domain bears
little information about its judgement on another domain of the same study,
which is consistent with the domain-specific nature of each RoB criterion.

Using the Kish~(1965) design-effect formula, the effective sample size is
\begin{equation}
  n_{\text{eff}} = \frac{n_{\text{cells}}}{1 + (k-1)\hat\rho}
                = \frac{1{,}589}{1 + 6 \times 0.044} \approx 1{,}257,
  \label{eq:neff}
\end{equation}
where $k = 7$ domains per study.

Wilson with $n_{\text{cells}}$ underestimates uncertainty by $\sim$10\% relative

because it discards the domain-level information and treats each study as a
single binary observation.

\subsubsection*{Why bootstrap was adopted for all metrics}

The numerical equivalence between Wilson~$n_{\text{eff}}$ and bootstrap for
accuracy confirms that the low ICC ($\hat\rho = 0.044$) makes the independence
assumption nearly tenable.
Nevertheless, bootstrap was used uniformly for three reasons.
First, Macro-F1 and Gwet's~AC1 are composite statistics with no closed-form
variance expression; bootstrap applies without modification.
Second, the paired-bootstrap procedure used for inference-mode $\Delta$ values
(Table~\ref{tab:inference}) jointly resamples both conditions, correctly
propagating correlation between them; Wilson intervals cannot be directly
adapted for paired differences of non-proportion statistics.
Third, a single method for all reported CIs ensures internal consistency and
simplifies interpretation.

\subsection*{S9\quad Expanded Formulas for Cohen's $\kappa$ and Gwet's AC1}
\label{sec:si-metrics}

Let $\mathbf{N} = (N_{ij})$ be the $K \times K$ confusion matrix ($K = 3$),
with rows indexed by predicted class $i$ and columns indexed by ground-truth
class $j$, $N = \sum_{i,j} N_{ij}$ the total number of evaluated cells, and
\begin{equation*}
  \hat{p}_{k,\text{model}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{K} N_{kj},
  \qquad
  \hat{p}_{k,\text{gt}}    = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{K} N_{ik}
\end{equation*}
the marginal proportions of class $k$ in the model predictions and ground
truth (row and column sums of $\mathbf{N}$ divided by $N$), as defined in
the Methods.

\textbf{Cohen's $\kappa$.}
The chance-expected agreement is
\begin{equation*}
  p_e^\kappa = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \hat{p}_{k,\text{model}} \cdot \hat{p}_{k,\text{gt}},
\end{equation*}
where each term $\hat{p}_{k,\text{model}} \cdot \hat{p}_{k,\text{gt}}$ is the
probability that both the model and ground truth independently assign class $k$
to a randomly selected cell.
Substituting into $\kappa = (p_o - p_e^\kappa)/(1 - p_e^\kappa)$ with
$p_o = \sum_k N_{kk}/N$ gives the equivalent form in raw counts:
\begin{equation*}
  \kappa =
    \frac{N\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{K} N_{kk}
        - \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{K}
            \Bigl(\sum_{j=1}^{K} N_{kj}\Bigr)\!\Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^{K} N_{ik}\Bigr)}
         {N^2
        - \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{K}
            \Bigl(\sum_{j=1}^{K} N_{kj}\Bigr)\!\Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^{K} N_{ik}\Bigr)}.
\end{equation*}

\textbf{Gwet's AC1.}
The mean marginal proportion for class $k$ is
\begin{equation*}
  \pi_k = \frac{\hat{p}_{k,\text{model}} + \hat{p}_{k,\text{gt}}}{2},
\end{equation*}
and the chance-agreement term is
\begin{equation*}
  p_e = \frac{1}{K-1}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k(1 - \pi_k).
\end{equation*}
AC1 $= (p_o - p_e)/(1 - p_e)$ follows with $p_o$ as above.

The key difference between the two coefficients lies in their chance terms:
$p_e^\kappa$ depends on the \emph{product} of the two marginals, making it
sensitive to imbalance in either rater's distribution; $p_e$ for AC1 depends
on the \emph{mean} marginal $\pi_k$, which is more stable when one rater
(here, the ground truth) has highly skewed class proportions.

\subsection*{S10\quad Prompt Used for Figure Description (VLM Pipeline)}
\label{sec:si-vlm-prompt}

During Stage~2 of the document processing pipeline
(Section~\ref{sec:pipeline}), each figure placeholder was submitted to
Gemini~2.5~Flash (\texttt{google/gemini-2.5-flash}) with the following
fixed prompt.
The system message was:

\small
\begin{verbatim}
You are converting a scientific figure into faithful Markdown text.
Describe only what is visibly present in the figure.
Do not infer unstated conclusions, do not mention OCR or the model,
and do not use JSON.
Return Markdown only.
\end{verbatim}
\normalsize

The user message was:

\small
\begin{verbatim}
Rewrite this scientific figure as Markdown so the image can be
removed from the extracted article.

Requirements:
- Preserve scientific meaning.
- Identify figure label and caption when visible or provided in context.
- Describe chart/figure type.
- Describe axes, units, legend, groups, markers, and error bars when visible.
- Summarize visible trends and approximate plotted relationships without
  inventing exact values.
- Mention unreadable or ambiguous areas explicitly.
- Keep the output concise but detailed enough to replace the image in
  the article.

Use this Markdown template:
### Figure Description
- source_image: `...`
- figure_label: ...
- caption: ...
- figure_type: ...
- axes_and_units: ...
- legend_and_groups: ...
- visual_summary: ...
- plotted_patterns: ...
- unreadable_or_uncertain: ...

Nearby article context:
{context}

Image path reference: {image_path}
\end{verbatim}
\normalsize

where \texttt{\{context\}} is the paragraph immediately surrounding the figure
in the extracted Markdown, and \texttt{\{image\_path\}} is the relative path
to the page image extracted by MinerU.
The prompt was used without modification for all figures across all 227 studies.

\subsection*{S11\quad Stratification by Publication Era}
\label{sec:si-era}

To investigate whether study characteristics associated with publication period
affect model performance, the 224 studies with a recorded publication year were
grouped into four eras: Pre-2000s (1960--1999; $n = 32$), 2000s (2000--2009;
$n = 44$), 2010s (2010--2019; $n = 105$), and 2020s (2020 onward; $n = 43$).
Figure~\ref{fig:si-decade-dist} shows the distribution of studies by publication
decade.
Figures~\ref{fig:si-era-agg} and~\ref{fig:si-era-models} show overall accuracy
per era aggregated across all eight models and per model, respectively.
Figure~\ref{fig:si-era-domain} shows accuracy per RoB domain across eras.
All confidence intervals are 95\% study-clustered bootstrap (5{,}000 iterations).

Point estimates suggest a modest trend of increasing accuracy from Pre-2000s
(56.0\%) to the 2020s (63.9\%); however, confidence intervals overlap across
all pairwise comparisons, including the most extreme (Pre-2000s vs.\ 2020s:
[50.2, 61.7] vs.\ [58.7, 69.3]), so a monotonic trend cannot be formally
established.
The wide interval for the Pre-2000s era reflects the small stratum size
($n = 32$) rather than greater variability in model performance.

Domain-level stratification reveals heterogeneous patterns.
D1 (random sequence generation) shows the clearest era effect, rising from
71\% in the Pre-2000s to 88\% in the 2020s, consistent with the progressive
adoption of CONSORT-mandated reporting of randomisation methods.
D3 (blinding of participants and personnel) shows the opposite trend,
declining from 64\% in the Pre-2000s to 53\% in the 2020s, which may reflect
the increasing prevalence of non-pharmacological and device trials in recent
decades, where blinding is structurally more complex to evaluate.
D7 (other bias) remains the lowest-performing domain across all eras
(38--45\%), with no discernible temporal trend, consistent with the
domain reflecting intrinsic criterion vagueness rather than a
reporting-period effect.

\subsection*{S12\quad Llama~3.3~70B: Output-Format Failures and Sidecar Recovery}
\label{sec:si-llama}

Llama~3.3~70B (\texttt{meta-llama/llama-3.3-70b-instruct}, accessed via OpenRouter)
has a context window of 131,072~tokens.
Reserving 1,024~tokens for model output and accounting for the system prompt
yields an effective input capacity of approximately 128,000~tokens for the
study evidence text.
Of the 227~studies, 74~(32.6\%) failed to produce a parseable response in the main
inference pipeline.
The dominant failure mode was not the domain judgment itself but the output format:
the model returned narrative, step-by-step prose rather than the required JSON object,
preventing automated extraction of the judgement.
These 74~studies were recovered via a dedicated sidecar pipeline that processed each
domain in a separate inference call and appended an explicit output-format instruction
to the original system prompt, requiring a single bare JSON object and forbidding
narrative or Markdown-formatted answers.
Up to three inference retries were allowed per domain; all 74~studies resolved to
valid assessments across all seven RoB domains.

One study, CD013595 (MAIA trial), required additional manual intervention beyond
the sidecar settings described above.
The MAIA document is a Cochrane Systematic Review report that consolidates five
separately published clinical trial reports into a single PDF, yielding an extracted
Markdown file of approximately 473,000~characters ($\sim$135,000~tokens)---exceeding
the effective input capacity even after output reservation was capped.
To bring the document within the context window, the following sections were manually
removed from the extracted Markdown copy: all five REFERENCES sections (the largest
contributor), acknowledgements, affiliations, author contributions,
competing-interests statements, and per-author conflict-of-interest blocks.
After cleaning, the document was reduced to approximately 384,000~characters
($\sim$110,000~tokens), placing it comfortably within the available input window.
The cleaned document was then processed through the same sidecar pipeline described
above.
All seven bias domains for MAIA were assessed successfully on the first or second
attempt, completing the 227-study benchmark for Llama~3.3~70B.
Two categories of content were removed.
\emph{Intentional removals} (applied uniformly across all five publications):
reference lists, acknowledgements, author affiliations, author contribution
statements, competing-interest disclosures, and other administrative sections.
\emph{Hard truncation}: after the above removals the document still exceeded the
context window; the final 384,000~characters were retained and the remainder
discarded.
The truncated portion falls entirely within the fifth publication (a secondary
analysis of patient-reported outcomes, HRQoL), and consists of the tail of a
supplementary subgroup table (EORTC~QLQ-C30 and EQ-5D-5L scores stratified by
age, ECOG performance status, and depth of response).
The Discussion section of the fifth publication---which contains the study
limitations and notes that participants were aware of their treatment
assignment---was retained in full.
No content bearing on the RoB domains (randomisation method, allocation
concealment, blinding procedures, primary outcome reporting, or trial
registration) was lost in either removal step.
